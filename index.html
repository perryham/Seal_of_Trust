A Data Governance Methodology

The Seal of Trust

A tiered certification framework that transforms data products from unmanaged liabilities into trusted, governed assets with measurable, defensible quality.

01 — The Problem

Most organizations govern data reactively, not systematically

Data governance programs frequently stall because they lack a visible, measurable mechanism for stakeholders to understand the quality and trustworthiness of the data they consume. Dashboards proliferate. KPIs are defined. But the fundamental question remains unanswered: how much should I trust this data?

Without a clear answer, business units make one of two choices: they trust everything uncritically, accepting risk they cannot quantify, or they trust nothing, duplicating effort and building shadow systems. Both outcomes erode governance credibility and waste organizational resources.

The Seal of Trust framework was designed to solve this problem by creating a universal, tiered language of data product maturity that any stakeholder can understand at a glance.
02 — The Framework

Four tiers. One universal language of trust.

Each data product, whether a dashboard, dataset, report, or automated feed, is assessed against defined criteria spanning ownership, documentation, quality measurement, and lifecycle management. Based on the assessment, the product receives a certification tier that communicates its governance maturity to all consumers.

B
Bronze
Identified
Data product is catalogued with a defined owner and basic metadata. Existence is acknowledged, but quality is unvalidated.
S
Silver
Documented
Lineage, definitions, and business rules are documented. Stakeholders understand what the data represents and where it originates.
G
Gold
Measured
Active quality rules are in place with automated monitoring. Issues are detected, tracked, and remediated through defined processes.
P
Platinum
Optimized
Continuous improvement is embedded. Quality metrics inform business decisions. The data product is a fully governed, trusted enterprise asset.
The power of tiered certification is not in the assessment itself, but in the shared vocabulary it creates. When a business leader sees "Gold," they know exactly what that means without reading a 40-page governance policy.
03 — Assessment Pillars

What gets measured at each tier

Certification is not a subjective judgment. Each tier is evaluated against six foundational pillars, with progressively rigorous expectations as the data product matures through the framework.

01
Ownership & Accountability
Every data product has a named data owner with defined responsibilities for quality, access, and lifecycle decisions. Ownership is not optional at any tier.
02
Documentation & Metadata
Business definitions, technical specifications, and contextual metadata are maintained in a central catalog. Consumers can find and understand data without tribal knowledge.
03
Lineage & Traceability
Source-to-consumption data flows are mapped and documented. Transformation logic is transparent and auditable for regulatory and operational confidence.
04
Quality Measurement
Defined quality dimensions (completeness, accuracy, timeliness, consistency, validity, uniqueness) are actively monitored with automated rules and thresholds.
05
Issue Management
Quality defects are logged, triaged, assigned, and remediated through a governed process. Root cause analysis drives systemic improvement, not one-off fixes.
06
Lifecycle Governance
Data products have defined creation, review, and retirement processes. Nothing persists ungoverned. Obsolete assets are decommissioned, not abandoned.
04 — How It Works

From assessment to certification

Step 1: Inventory & Nomination

Data products are identified, catalogued, and nominated for certification by their owners or by the governance team during enterprise data discovery exercises. Each nominated product enters the assessment pipeline with a baseline metadata record.

Step 2: Pillar Assessment

Each product is evaluated against the six pillars using a standardized rubric. Evidence is collected, not assumed. Documentation must exist, quality rules must be running, ownership must be formalized. The assessment produces a score mapped to the four-tier model.

Step 3: Certification & Communication

The data product receives its tier designation, which is published in the data catalog and, where applicable, displayed directly on dashboards, reports, or data product landing pages. Stakeholders see the trust level before they consume the data.

Step 4: Continuous Reassessment

Certification is not permanent. Products are reassessed on a defined cadence or triggered by material changes (source migration, schema changes, ownership transitions). Tiers can rise or fall based on current evidence, ensuring the framework reflects reality, not history.

06 — Applicability

Where this framework fits

The Seal of Trust is domain-agnostic by design. It has been applied to operational technology data but is equally applicable to any environment where data products serve business decisions and require governed trust signals.

Financial Services & Regulated Industries

Regulatory frameworks like OSFI E-23 (Model Risk Management) and E-21 (Operational Risk) require organizations to demonstrate data quality, lineage, and governance maturity. Tiered certification provides auditable evidence of compliance readiness and a clear maturity roadmap for regulators.

Healthcare & Public Sector

Privacy-sensitive environments benefit from the framework's emphasis on ownership, lifecycle governance, and documented handling procedures. Certification tiers communicate data readiness for reporting, analytics, and inter-agency data sharing.

Technology & SaaS

Product teams shipping data-driven features need confidence in the datasets powering their models and analytics. The framework integrates into CI/CD pipelines and data platform operations, providing quality gates that scale with engineering velocity.

07 — Design Principles

What makes it work

Visibility over complexity

Governance frameworks fail when they are invisible to the people who need them most. The tiered model succeeds because it is immediately legible to non-technical stakeholders. A business director does not need to understand data quality dimensions to understand what "Silver" means.

Evidence over assertion

Certification is earned through demonstrated compliance, not self-reported status. Quality rules must be running. Documentation must exist in the catalog. Ownership must be formalized. The framework does not accept "we plan to" as a substitute for "we have."

Progression over perfection

Bronze is not failure. It is the first step. The framework is designed to encourage incremental improvement rather than demand immediate Platinum maturity, which would discourage participation and undermine adoption.

Accountability over abstraction

Every data product has a named owner. Every quality issue has an assigned resolver. Every tier has a defined meaning. The framework eliminates ambiguity, which is the primary enemy of governance adoption.
